**Saurav Bhattarai** is a graduate research assistant at Jackson State University and an ORISE Fellow at the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers' Engineer Research and Development Center. **GeoClimate-Fetcher** is an open-source, web-based platform that democratizes access to planetary-scale environmental data. Built on Google Earth Engine's STAC API with access to 900+ satellite and climate datasets, it enables researchers, educators, and communities to analyze climate patterns, assess water hazards, and visualize environmental changes without requiring advanced programming expertise. The platform transforms complex Earth observation data into actionable insights for climate resilience and informed decision-making.

### About GeoClimate-Fetcher

**GeoClimate-Fetcher** (officially the **GeoClimate Intelligence Platform**) is an open-source, web-based framework for environmental data analysis that bridges the gap between massive Earth observation datasets and the people who need them most—researchers, educators, students, water managers, and community planners.

Built on Google Earth Engine's STAC API, the platform provides seamless access to over 900 satellite-based and climate datasets—including NASA's Earth observations, NOAA climate products, USGS land cover data, and ESA Copernicus missions—covering decades of environmental monitoring from local to global scales.

**What makes it unique:**
- **No coding required**: Intuitive web interface powered by Python and Streamlit
- **Comprehensive modules**:
  - *GeoData Explorer* for browsing and downloading from 900+ datasets
  - *Climate Analytics* for analyzing ETCCDI climate indices and extremes
  - *Hydrology Analyzer* for precipitation analysis and watershed studies
  - *Product Selector* for comparing gridded datasets with station observations
- **Publication-ready outputs**: Interactive visualizations, statistical analyses, and downloadable datasets
- **Education-focused**: Actively used in university courses to train the next generation of environmental scientists
- **Community-driven**: Open-source architecture allowing customization and contributions

The platform has been peer-reviewed and published in *Environmental Modelling & Software* (2025), validating its scientific rigor and practical utility. It represents a new paradigm in environmental data accessibility—moving from "data exists but is hard to use" to "data is accessible and immediately actionable."

GeoClimate-Fetcher is being used for real-world applications including:
- Urban heat island monitoring in cities across the U.S.
- Flood risk assessment for vulnerable coastal communities
- Drought monitoring for agricultural regions
- Climate change impact assessment for infrastructure planning
- Environmental justice research examining disparities in climate hazard exposure

### About GeoClimate-Fetcher: The Platform Revolution

The **GeoClimate Intelligence Platform** (GeoClimate-Fetcher) represents Saurav's vision to democratize environmental data science. Born from the recognition that vast amounts of satellite and climate data exist but remain inaccessible to those who need them most—community planners, educators, water managers, and researchers without advanced programming skills—the platform embodies a fundamental shift in how we interact with Earth observation data.

**The Technical Foundation:** Built on Google Earth Engine's STAC (SpatioTemporal Asset Catalog) API using Python and Streamlit, the platform provides seamless access to over 900 satellite-based and climate datasets spanning multiple decades and covering local to global scales. This includes:
- **NASA Earth Observations**: MODIS, Landsat, GPM precipitation, SRTM elevation, and more
- **NOAA Climate Products**: ERA5 reanalysis, CHIRPS precipitation, CPC data
- **USGS Land Resources**: National Land Cover Database, dynamic surface water
- **ESA Copernicus Missions**: Sentinel-2 imagery and derived products
- **Specialized Climate Datasets**: Daymet, gridMET, PRISM, and numerous regional products

**The Four Pillars:** The platform consists of four integrated modules, each designed for specific use cases:

1. **GeoData Explorer** - The gateway to 900+ datasets. Users can search by keywords, filter by theme (climate, land cover, weather, vegetation, etc.), select any area of interest globally, choose bands and date ranges, and download data in multiple formats (GeoTIFF, CSV, NetCDF). The interactive map preview allows users to visualize data before downloading, ensuring they're getting exactly what they need.

2. **Climate Analytics** - A specialized module for analyzing ETCCDI (Expert Team on Climate Change Detection and Indices) climate indices. Users can compute and visualize 27 different climate indices including extreme temperature metrics (heat waves, cold snaps, tropical nights), precipitation extremes (consecutive wet/dry days, heavy precipitation events), and compound indices. The module generates time series analyses, statistical summaries, and publication-ready visualizations—transforming months of programming work into minutes of point-and-click analysis.

3. **Hydrology Analyzer** - Focused on precipitation analysis for watershed studies, this module provides comprehensive tools for analyzing 9 different precipitation datasets (ERA5-Land, gridMET, CHIRPS, GPM IMERG, Daymet, PRISM, and others). Users can perform temporal aggregation, spatial statistics, trend analysis, and generate hydrologically-relevant outputs for rainfall-runoff modeling or flood assessment. The module is actively used in university hydrology courses for teaching precipitation analysis.

4. **Product Selector** - A unique module that helps researchers compare gridded climate datasets with ground station observations from the Meteostat network. This is crucial for dataset validation, selecting optimal data sources for a region, and understanding the strengths and limitations of different climate products. Users can identify which satellite or reanalysis product best represents local conditions before committing to large-scale analyses.

**The Impact Story:** What makes GeoClimate-Fetcher truly revolutionary isn't just its technical capabilities—it's the barriers it removes. A high school teacher in Mississippi can now access the same NASA satellite data that climate researchers use. A community planner in a small coastal town can assess flood risk under different climate scenarios without hiring expensive consultants. An undergraduate student in an HBCU can perform cutting-edge climate analysis without needing to learn complex programming languages first.

The platform is actively being used for:
